A note from the founder

The eye of an educator. The heart of a counsellor.

For a decade I sat with children who could not yet name what they were carrying. Robora is what I built when I learned to listen.

I started Robora because I realised that no wealth could mask trauma. When children act out or withdraw, it is the pain inside speaking — and that pain has a name, a story, and a way home.

Before I was a counsellor I was a teacher. For nearly twelve years I stood in classrooms across Nigeria and watched children who were brilliant, exhausted, frightened, and quietly carrying things no child should carry alone. I saw the boy who never raised his hand. The girl who couldn't sit still. The teenager who slept through every lesson. The straight-A student whose eyes had gone somewhere else.

What teachers are taught to call behaviour, I learned to read as a language. Acting out, shutting down, perfectionism, anger — these are not character flaws. They are signals. And when no one translates them, children grow into adults who still do not know what they were trying to say.

Why Robora exists

Robora draws its name from the Latin roborare — to strengthen. Our practice is built on a simple conviction: that every child carries an unspoken resilience, and our work is to help it find its voice.

Like the oak whose roots run deep before any branch reaches the sky, healing is not a performance. It is a quiet, patient, structural work. We meet families where the silence has settled, where conflict has learned the shape of the home, where a child is asking for help in the only language they know. And we begin.

The work I do, and why

I am a certified family therapist and educator with over a decade of cross-cultural work — Nigeria, the United Kingdom, and the United States. My practice sits at the meeting place of education and emotional healing: clinical insight, classroom wisdom, and where it is welcomed, a quiet thread of faith.

I work with children from age six, teenagers in transition, parents navigating the long road of attachment repair, and the schools and churches that hold them. I lead workshops for educators on trauma-informed teaching. I sit with mothers who are quietly drowning. I sit with teenagers who have stopped speaking to their parents. I sit with children who have not yet found the words.

What I believe

  • Behaviour is communication. Always. We listen first, interpret slowly, and never punish what we have not yet understood.
  • Healing is relational. No one heals in isolation. The work is held by trusted adults — parents, teachers, clergy, community.
  • Faith is not coercion. For families who hold one, faith is a resource. For those who do not, the work stands on its own. We meet you where you are.
  • Children are not small adults. The therapy room must speak their language — through play, through art, through stories — long before it asks them to speak ours.

A short note on home

I am Yoruba, born and raised in Lagos, with the inheritance of a culture that has always known how to hold its young. Some of what I do is new — trauma-informed practice, attachment theory, regulation skills. Some of it is older than I am: the wisdom of iya who watched and waited, of baba who told the story until the child could tell it back. I do not separate these. The work belongs to both.

If you are a parent reading this and your hands are tired — welcome. If you are a school leader and your staff are running on fumes — welcome. If you are a pastor and your families are in pieces — welcome. There is room here, and there is work to do.

— Joanne
