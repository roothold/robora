What stays in the room

Confidentiality notice.

Therapy depends on trust. Trust depends on knowing the limits, before you need to.

This notice describes what is kept confidential in our work, what is not, and the special considerations that apply when the client is a child or teenager. Please read it before your first session, and ask us about anything that is unclear.

This notice is provided for transparency. It is not a substitute for legal or clinical advice. Robora reviews this notice annually with a clinical supervisor and a legal advisor, and we will tell you if it has changed since you became a client.

The general rule

What you tell us in session is confidential. We do not discuss your name, your story, or anything you bring to the room with anyone outside our practice — not in conversation, not on social media, not at gatherings. This is the foundation of the work.

The exceptions

There are a small number of situations in which we are professionally and legally obliged to share information without your consent. These are not exceptions we go looking for — they are exceptions we are bound by.

1. Risk of serious harm to yourself or others

If you tell us — or we have strong reason to believe — that you intend to seriously harm yourself or another person, we are obliged to take steps to protect life. Where possible, we will discuss this with you first and involve you in the decision. Where it is not possible, we will act to keep someone safe and tell you what we have done as soon as we can.

2. Suspected abuse of a child or vulnerable adult

If, in the course of our work, we become aware of ongoing abuse, neglect, or exploitation of a child or vulnerable adult, we have a mandatory duty to report this to the appropriate authorities under Nigerian child protection law and our professional code. This is not optional. We tell you what we are doing and why, unless doing so would put a child at greater risk.

3. Court orders

If a court of competent jurisdiction issues a valid order requiring us to disclose specific information from our records, we are required to comply. We will resist any disclosure that goes beyond what is strictly required, and we will tell you that an order has been received unless we are forbidden by the order itself.

4. Clinical supervision

Like all good practitioners, Joanne meets regularly with a clinical supervisor — another qualified counsellor — to reflect on the work. In supervision, identifying details (names, places, distinguishing circumstances) are removed. Supervision is bound by the same confidentiality as therapy itself.

Working with minors

When the client is a child or teenager, confidentiality is held with particular care. Children and parents both have legitimate interests, and we work hard to honour both.

What parents are told

Parents are kept informed about general progress, themes that arise, and any clinical decisions about the work. They are not given verbatim accounts of what their child has said. The therapy room must be a place where a child can say difficult things without fearing they will be reported home.

What parents are not told

Specific disclosures from the child — about feelings, friends, faith, identity, or family — are held confidentially unless one of the four exceptions above applies. We will tell the child clearly, in their language, what we will and will not share.

Where this becomes complicated

Sometimes a child discloses something that does not meet the threshold for mandatory reporting, but that we believe a parent should know. In those cases we work with the child to find a way for them to tell their parent — supported, prepared, with us present if needed. We do not go around them.

The non-secret-keeping rule

We do not promise children that we will keep secrets from their parents. We do promise that we will tell them, before anything is shared, what is being shared and why. This protects both the trust of the therapy room and the integrity of the family.

Records and notes

Our session notes are confidential records. They are stored as described in our privacy policy. You may request a copy of your own records at any time. Parents may request a copy of their child's records, with the considerations above.

School and church partnerships

When Robora works inside a school or church, the same confidentiality rules apply with a few additions. Children and adults speaking with us through a school or church program are still our clients, not the institution's. The institution receives general feedback about themes and program impact — not individual disclosures. We are explicit about this with everyone before the work begins.

Group sessions and workshops

In group settings, we cannot guarantee what other participants will or will not share outside the room. We ask everyone to honour group confidentiality, and we make clear that this is an honour-based agreement, not a clinical guarantee.

If something feels unclear

If at any point you are unsure what is confidential and what is not — ask. There is no question too small. The clarity protects both of us, and it protects the work.

Before we begin

If anything in this notice worries you, write to us.

We would rather hear your concern at the threshold than after a session. Confidentiality is a relationship, not a document.
